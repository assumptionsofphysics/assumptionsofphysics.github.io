Assumptions of Physics

Putting physics first by deriving the existing mathematical frameworks from simple physical assumptions

The fundamental questions

While some branches of fundamental physics (such as Newtonian mechanics, thermodynamics and special relativity) are founded on physical laws or principles, others (such as Hamiltonian, Lagrangian and Quantum mechanics) are founded on their respective mathematical frameworks. If one is to ask "what is a Hamiltonian system?" the only answer is "one that is described by Hamilton's equations".

The aim of this work is to re-found the well established mathematical frameworks starting from physical assumptions that characterize the system under study. This provides a more physically meaningful approach to the theory and provides better intuitive understanding of the mathematical tools.

Assumptions and consequences

From each physical assumption we can derive a set of physical and mathematical consequences. This allows us to better understand what Hamiltonian/Lagrangian Classical/Quantum systems are.

Assumption Consequence
I Determinism and reversibility. Given the present state of the system under study, all future and past states are uniquely identified. Dynamical system. State space is a Hausdorff, second countable topological space. Deterministic and reversible evolution is a self-homeomorphism. Trajectories in state space are continuous.
II.a Classical assumption of infinitesimal reducibility. The system under study is made of a homogeneous material that can be decomposed into infinitesimal parts. Giving the state of the whole system is equivalent to giving the state of each infinitesimal part. The evolution of each part is deterministic and reversible. Classical Hamiltonian particle mechanics. The state space of the homogeneous material is isomorphic to the set of Lebesgue integrable continuous real functions that describe the distribution of the material of the states of the infinitesimal parts. The state space of the infinitesimal parts (i.e. phase space) is the cotangent bundle of the manifold that determines the system of units. On phase space the canonical two-form is a metric that returns the number of possibilities for each degree of freedom. Each infinitesimal part evolves according to Hamilton's equations.
II.b Quantum assumption of irreducibility.The system under study is made of a homogeneous material that can be decomposed into infinitesimal parts. Giving the state of the whole system does not provide any description for the infinitesimal parts. The evolution of the system as a whole is deterministic and reversible but not at the level of each infinitesimal part. Quantum Hamiltonian particle mechanics. The state space of the homogeneous material is isomorphic to the set of Lebesgue square-integrable continuous complex functions that describe the distribution of material in terms of two independent stochastic variables. The whole system evolves according to the Schroedinger equation.
III Kinematic equivalence. For the system under study, the kinematics (i.e. trajectories in physical space-time) and the dynamics (i.e. trajectories in state space) are equivalent. Giving a set of initial conditions, such as position and velocity, is equivalent to giving the initial state. Lagrangian particle mechanics with scalar/vector potential forces. The Hamiltonian is limited to the class for massive particles moving under forces described by scalar or vector potentials. We can define a Legendre transformation for the Hamiltonian.
